# Interview Prep: Carle Health - Sr Finance Business Analyst (Recruiter Phone Screen)

Prepared 2026-06-28. Stage: **recruiter phone screen**. A recruiter screen is mostly narrative,
fit, logistics, and comp. It is not a deep technical grilling. Goal: come across as clearly
qualified, easy to work with, and reasonable on comp/logistics so they advance you to the
hiring manager. The STAR bank at the bottom is for the 1-2 behavioral questions that may come up
and for the hiring-manager round after this.

---

## 1. Recruiter-screen essentials (lead with these)

### Tell me about yourself (60-90 sec)
"I'm a finance professional with over ten years in corporate accounting and FP&A, most recently as
a Senior Financial Analyst at SSA Marine Terminals. My niche is the overlap between finance and the
systems finance runs on. I've owned monthly reporting, budget and forecast models, and variance
analysis, and I led our finance team through a full ERP migration off JD Edwards onto NetSuite and
Hyperion, handling the testing, data-integrity validation, and end-user support. I really enjoy
being the person who connects the finance users, IT, and vendors and makes the systems actually
work. That's exactly why this Finance Business Analyst role caught my eye."

### Why this role / why Carle
- The role is a near-exact match to what I already do best: report writing, FIS testing and
  upgrades, data integrity, process improvement, and acting as the liaison across finance, IT,
  and vendors.
- I want to go deeper on the systems side of finance, and this role is built around that.
- Carle is a major, stable employer rooted in central Illinois, where I'm based. Mission-driven
  healthcare with room for long-term growth appeals to me.

### Why are you looking / status (PREPARE THIS HONESTLY)
Your SSA Marine role shows as ending May 2026, so the recruiter will ask. Have a calm, neutral
one-liner ready. Pick the true version:
- If the role ended (layoff/restructure): "My position at SSA was eliminated in a restructuring
  this spring, so I'm actively looking for my next step, and I'm being deliberate about finding
  the right systems-focused finance role." Keep it blameless and brief, then pivot to enthusiasm.
- If you left voluntarily / it's winding down: state that plainly and pivot.
Do not over-explain. One or two sentences, then redirect to why Carle.

### Salary expectations (DECIDE YOUR NUMBER BEFORE THE CALL)
The posted band is **$33.72-$58.00/hour** (~$70k-$121k/yr). This is non-exempt/hourly and below a
typical senior-FP&A salary. The recruiter will likely ask your expectations or confirm the band.
- Recommended posture: "Based on my experience I'd be targeting the upper half of the posted range.
  Can you tell me where this role typically lands for someone with 10+ years and ERP-implementation
  experience?" That anchors high, stays inside their band, and makes them show their hand.
- Know your personal floor before the call. If the midpoint (~$95k) clears it, this is workable.
  If only the top of the band clears it, say so politely now rather than after three rounds.
- Don't volunteer your old SSA salary unless asked; Illinois lets you decline to state prior pay.

### Logistics (have clean answers ready)
- **On-site, Champaign, Day shift, M-F 40 hrs.** You're in Watseka (~45-50 min / ~45 mi each way).
  Be ready to confirm you're comfortable with the commute, or ask if there's any hybrid
  flexibility. Don't raise it as a problem; state it as settled if you're OK with it.
- Availability to start, and availability for next interview rounds.
- Work authorization (they use E-Verify): confirm you're authorized to work in the US.

### Workday gap (only if asked about tools)
"I haven't used Workday specifically; my systems are NetSuite, Hyperion/PBCS, and JD Edwards. But
I've led a full ERP migration end to end, so picking up a new financial system's interface is
something I ramp on quickly. The transferable part, the testing discipline, data validation, and
working across finance, IT, and vendors, is exactly what I've done."

### 3-4 questions to ask the recruiter
1. What does the interview process look like after this, and who would I meet?
2. What's the team structure, and who would I report to?
3. What's driving the opening, new role or a backfill?
4. Is the role strictly on-site, or is there any hybrid flexibility?
5. What does success in the first 6-12 months look like for this role?

## 2. STAR bank (for behavioral questions + the hiring-manager round)

Each story is tagged to the Carle JD responsibility it proves. Keep each to ~60-90 seconds out loud.

### STAR 1 - Leading an FIS/ERP system change (data integrity + testing + implementation)
Proves: "review, testing and implementation of FIS upgrades/patches"; "ensuring data integrity";
"document process and results."
- **S:** At SSA Marine, our finance team was running on JD Edwards, which was being retired.
- **T:** I was responsible for getting the finance side onto NetSuite and Oracle Hyperion/PBCS and
  making sure the data migrated clean.
- **A:** I led the system testing, validated migrated data against the legacy records to confirm
  integrity, ran user-acceptance testing with the finance team, and acted as the liaison between
  finance, IT, and the vendor to resolve issues as they surfaced.
- **R:** Finance cut over to the new systems with validated data and minimal disruption, and I
  became the go-to support contact for end users afterward.

### STAR 2 - Liaison / troubleshooting across users, IT, and vendors
Proves: "liaison support between functional end users, external vendors and local IT";
"researching and resolving FIS problems."
- **S:** After the migration, finance users would hit reporting and system issues they couldn't
  resolve on their own.
- **T:** I was the bridge between the finance end users, internal IT, and the external vendors.
- **A:** I'd translate the finance problem into technical terms, reproduce and research the issue,
  pull in IT or the vendor when needed, and follow it through to a fix, then explain the resolution
  back to the users in plain language.
- **R:** Issues got resolved faster because there was a single owner who spoke both finance and
  systems, and users trusted the process.

### STAR 3 - Process improvement / automation
Proves: "identifying process improvement opportunities"; "recommend solutions or alternate methods."
- **S:** Our month-end reporting and a payroll reconciliation across 15 cost centers were manual
  and slow.
- **T:** I wanted to cut the cycle time and reduce manual error.
- **A:** I identified the bottleneck, built automated routines in Power Query for the month-end
  reporting, and streamlined the payroll reconciliation process.
- **R:** I shortened the payroll reconciliation cycle by a full week and made month-end reporting
  repeatable instead of hand-built each period.

### STAR 4 - Reporting and analysis under deadline
Proves: "generates standard and ad hoc reports and finance metrics; research, review, compile,
and analyze financial data."
- **S:** Management and executives needed both routine and one-off financial answers on tight
  timelines.
- **T:** I owned the reporting that informed their decisions.
- **A:** I generated standard and ad hoc reports and finance metrics in NetSuite, produced monthly
  P&L for 15 cost centers with budget-vs-actual variance analysis, and partnered with the CFO on
  the variance story behind the numbers.
- **R:** Leaders got accurate, on-time numbers they could act on, and I was trusted with the
  executive- and board-level reporting.

### STAR 5 - Catching/resolving a data or reporting problem (if asked about a mistake/challenge)
Proves: "unexpected results or process flaws"; data integrity.
- **S:** During reporting/close, I'd periodically find numbers that didn't tie out as expected.
- **T:** I had to find the root cause before the reports went to management.
- **A:** I'd trace the variance back through the system, reconcile against source data, and figure
  out whether it was a data, configuration, or process issue, then fix the source rather than just
  patching the output.
- **R:** Reporting stayed reliable, and I built a reputation for catching issues before they
  reached leadership.
